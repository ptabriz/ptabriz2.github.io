Exploring perceived restoration potential of urban green enclosure through immersive virtual environments

Posted on November 8, 2017

Intro

Small green urban spaces can be restorative because it conceals undesired environmental cues and accommodates an intimate personal space or time, “a way of regaining an interior world”, and yet are also feared because they can imply lack of control and scape. This tension between two polarities motivated this research to explore the relationships between spatial enclosure and perceptions of restorativeness and safety, and how embedded context (natural/urban) may interact with these hypothetical relationships. Does in urban contexts, spaces surrounded by impermeable landscape elements are judged as more restorative than open spaces? How context type influences the hypothesized effect of enclosure on safety and restoration perception?

Method

Photorealistic 360o panoramas taken from two urban settings (a plaza and a park) were digitally manipulated to obtain 18 immersive virtual environment (IVE) stimuli depicting variations of spatial arrangement and permeability of vegetation. Using a head-mounted display, 87 participants viewed the IVEs and rated each on perceived restorativeness and perceived safety using a joystick controller. The data were examined using analysis of variance and mediation analysis.

Results

Findings show that overall perceived restorativeness was higher in the park setting, but perceived safety was higher in the plaza setting. Increasing enclosure in plaza setting significantly improved perceived restorativeness but did not affect perceived safety, whereas in park setting enclosure negatively influenced both perceived restorativeness and perceived safety. Mediation analysis indicated that feelings of safety significantly influence the causal relationships between enclosure and perceived restorativeness with more pronounced effect in park setting. These results suggest that small variations in number and arrangement of vegetation enhance or suppress restorative quality of urban settings, and that the direction and magnitude of these effects are sensitive to the situated context, and are mediated through perceived safety.
